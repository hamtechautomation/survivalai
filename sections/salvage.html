1. The Salvage Mindset & Priorities

Salvage is not looting — in a genuine long-term collapse it is recycling with a purpose, and in anything short of that it is theft. The legal and ethical line matters: salvage from what is truly abandoned, your own property, or with permission. That said, the skill of seeing machines as organs — motors, bearings, wire, switches, tanks — is worth building now.

Priorities change with time. In the first weeks, consumables matter most; after a year, it's rotating machinery and copper.

PhaseTake firstWhy
Early (weeks)Fuel, batteries, wire, hand tools, fasteners, oils & lubricants, filtersConsumables disappear fastest and degrade in place
Middle (months)Motors, alternators, pumps, bearings, belts, switches, breakers, tanksThe building blocks of every rebuilt system
Late (years)Structural steel, glass, roofing, piping, transformer cores, magnet wireFabrication feedstock once machines are gone

Rules that keep salvage useful: take the whole assembly when you can (a motor with its mount, capacitor and wiring beats a bare motor); label everything at the point of removal (masking tape + pencil); photograph or sketch before disconnecting; and store like with like — a box of mixed fasteners is a box of nothing.

3. Safe Stripping — What Can Kill You

☠️
The five classic salvage killers

1. Charged capacitors — microwave HV caps and motor-start caps hold lethal charge for days after unplugging. Discharge through an insulated-handle screwdriver across the terminals (expect a loud snap), or better, a 10kΩ resistor on leads. Then short the terminals with wire while you work.
2. Airbags & seat-belt pretensioners — pyrotechnic charges. Never cut, drill or heat a steering-wheel hub or B-pillar module. Disconnect the car battery and wait 15 minutes before touching any airbag wiring (usually yellow looms).
3. Fuel tanks & drums — empty tanks are the dangerous ones: vapour + spark = bomb. Never cut, grind or weld a tank that ever held fuel until it has been washed out and filled with water or purged. See Metallurgy before hot work.
4. Refrigerant & pressurised systems — AC lines, fridge circuits and hydraulic accumulators are under pressure. Crack fittings slowly, wear eye protection, keep refrigerant away from flame (phosgene-like decomposition products).
5. Stored spring energy — garage-door springs, gas struts, and clutch assemblies release violently. If you don't know how it's tensioned, don't unbolt it.

Slower killers to respect: asbestos (pre-1990 buildings — lagging, ceiling tiles, brake pads: wet it, don't grind it, mask up), lead (paint dust, battery paste — wash hands, no food nearby), CRT televisions (implosion hazard + charged anode), and mains feed-back — treat every wire in a building as live until you have personally proven it dead at the point of work (see the electrical safety rules in Energy §10).

4. The Salvage Toolkit

A dedicated grab-bag makes the difference between stripping a site in an hour and giving up. Build it now, keep it packed:

  • Drivers & sockets: multi-bit driver (incl. Torx & security bits — appliances love them), 8–19 mm sockets + ratchet, adjustable wrench, mole grips/vice-grips.
  • Cutting: hacksaw + spare blades, side cutters, cable shears, utility knife, small pry bar, cold chisel, bolt croppers if you can carry them.
  • Electrical: multimeter (the single most valuable salvage tool), insulated screwdriver, electrical tape, a 10kΩ bleed resistor on leads, wire strippers.
  • Handling: heavy gloves + thin mechanic's gloves, safety glasses, headtorch, masking tape + pencil (labelling), zip-lock bags for fasteners, a magnet on a string (finds steel, sorts metals).
  • Fluids: penetrating oil, siphon hose, drain pan, sealable jugs — the oils and fuels you drain are salvage too.

6. Generator Maintenance

A portable generator dies from neglect far more often than from wear. The engine is a lawnmower engine; the electrical end is nearly maintenance-free. Keep the engine alive and the set lives for decades. (Deep reference: TM 5-685, bundled.)

The maintenance schedule that matters

IntervalTaskNotes
Every runCheck oil, check air filter, look for leaksSmall engines burn oil quietly; low oil kills them in minutes
First 20–25 h (new/rebuilt)First oil changeFlushes break-in metal
Every 50–100 hOil change (SAE 30 warm climate; 10W-30 variable)Drain hot. If no new oil: settle used oil weeks, decant the top — worse than new, better than none
Every 100 hAir filter service; spark plug check (gap ≈ 0.7–0.8 mm / 0.028–0.031")Foam pre-filters wash in soapy water, dry, re-oil lightly
Every 300 h / yearlyValve clearance check, fuel filter/sediment bowl clean, cooling fins brushed cleanClogged fins overheat and warp heads on air-cooled engines
Monthly (stored)Run 20–30 min under load (~50%)No-load running glazes cylinders and wets plugs; load exercise keeps everything sealed

Storage that doesn't wreck it

Fuel off, carb dry: Turn the fuel valve off and run the carburettor dry, or drain the float bowl. Gummed carburettors are the #1 cause of "generator won't start."
Stabilise the tank: If storing with fuel, add stabiliser and run 5 minutes to get treated fuel through the carb (see §15).
Fog the cylinder: Pull the plug, pour in a teaspoon of engine oil, pull the cord slowly twice, refit the plug. Stops bore rust.
Store dry and covered, cord in a relaxed position, battery (electric start) disconnected and topped up quarterly.
⚠️
Generator placement kills more people than generator failure

Never run one in a garage, shed, or near windows/vents — carbon monoxide is odourless and lethal. Outside only, exhaust pointed away, 6 m from openings. Refuel only when off and cool.

7. Generator Troubleshooting & Rebuild

Split every problem in two: engine side (won't start / runs badly) and electrical side (runs fine, no power out). Engine diagnosis is the spark–fuel–air–compression drill covered in Vehicles §3 — it applies unchanged.

Engine runs, no output — in order of likelihood

1. Tripped breaker / dead outlet: Check the set's own breakers and test at multiple outlets with a meter. Obvious, and still the most common.
2. Lost residual magnetism ("needs flashing"): A generator bootstraps from a whisper of leftover magnetism in the rotor. Long storage, or running with no load until fuel-out, loses it. Symptom: engine fine, 0–2 V output. Fix — field flashing: with the set running, touch a 12 V battery across the brush leads (AVR disconnected; correct polarity: + to +) for 1–2 seconds through a fused lead — output should snap up immediately. On brushless sets, an electric drill spun backwards into an outlet while running can re-excite it (its motor acts as a magnetised generator feeding the windings).
3. AVR failure: The automatic voltage regulator is the most-failed electrical part. Symptoms: no output, half voltage, or wild swings. Test by substitution if you have a spare (they're small and cheap — stock two), or bypass briefly with a 12 V battery on the field to prove the windings are good.
4. Worn brushes / dirty slip rings: Brushes shorter than ~6 mm, or rings blackened — pull the end cover, clean the rings with fine abrasive (never file), replace or extend brushes. Brush sets from other machines can often be sanded to fit.
5. Capacitor (brushless sets): Condenser-excited brushless generators use a run capacitor — if output is dead or ~half voltage, swap the capacitor (match µF; borrow from a motor of similar size).
6. Windings: Meter the stator: continuity on each winding, no continuity to the frame. Open or grounded windings mean a rewind — magnet wire from microwave transformers and motor stators can rebuild small rotors, but it is patient work. That's a last resort.

Diesel generator specifics

  • Air in the fuel = no start. After any filter change or run-dry: crack the injector line nuts, crank until fuel weeps out clean, retighten. Most small diesels have a hand-primer pump on the filter housing — pump until firm.
  • Glow plugs: hard cold-starting → test each plug for continuity; they fail one at a time.
  • Water and "diesel bug": drain the water separator regularly; microbial growth in stored diesel clogs filters — treat stored fuel with biocide and keep tanks full to limit condensation (§15).
  • Injectors: white smoke + knock on one cylinder → crack each injector line in turn while running; the line that makes no difference feeds the dead injector. Clean tips in solvent; replacement beats rebuilding without a pop tester.
  • Diesels last. A cared-for small diesel outlives its owner: clean fuel, clean air, clean oil is 90% of it.

8. Building a Generator from a Car Alternator

Every dead car donates a 12–14 V, 40–120 A generator. Spin it fast enough and excite its field, and you have a battery charger for any 12 V bank — which through an inverter is household power.

1. Choose the drive: Any small engine (mower, tiller) of 2 hp+, a bicycle on a stand for light charging, or a water wheel (Power Generation). Alternators want 2,000–6,000 RPM at the pulley.
2. Gear it up: Small engines run ~3,600 RPM. Use a drive pulley 2–3× the alternator pulley diameter to reach ~7,000+ alternator RPM at full output — or accept lower output at 1:1. V-belts from the same donor car work.
3. Excite the field: Most alternators need the field fed to start charging. Simplest wiring: battery + → dash-lamp wire (the small terminal, via a 3–5 W bulb) → alternator; the bulb lights when not charging and dies when charging — a free status light. Self-exciting (one-wire) alternators skip this but need a rev blip to wake up.
4. Wire the output: Big output stud → battery + through a fuse (size to alternator rating); frame → battery −. Charge into a battery, never into electronics directly — the battery smooths the output.
5. Mount it stiff: Belt tension needs a solid base — hardwood plank or angle-iron frame, engine and alternator bolted, tension by sliding the alternator in slots (exactly as the car did it).

Realistic expectations: 400–700 W continuous from a mower-engine rig — enough to charge a battery bank a few hours a day, run lights, radios and charging (see the load table in Energy). For 230/120 V AC, add an inverter sized to your loads.

9. Salvaging & Rebuilding Batteries

Batteries are the scarcest link in any post-grid power system — and the most salvageable. Two families matter: lead-acid (cars, trucks, UPS, solar banks) and lithium (laptops, power tools, e-bikes).

Lead-acid: triage first

Resting voltage (12 V battery, 12+ h off charge)StateVerdict
12.6–12.9 VFull, healthyUse it
12.0–12.4 VPart-dischargedCharge and load-test
10.5–11.9 VDeeply discharged / sulfatingRecovery candidate — slow charge + desulfation
<10.5 V, or exactly ~10.6 V that won't riseLikely dead cell (shorted plate)Scrap — one dead cell can't be fixed from outside

Recovery routine: top up any low cells with distilled/rain water only (never acid — the acid never left), then charge long and slow (C/20 — e.g. 2–3 A for a car battery) for 24–48 h. If a hydrometer reads all cells within 0.030 specific gravity of each other (healthy full ≈ 1.265), it lives. A deliberate equalisation charge — controlled overcharge at ~15.5 V for 2–4 h on flooded batteries only, caps off, well ventilated — stirs stratified acid and knocks light sulfation off plates. Pulse desulfators genuinely help mildly sulfated batteries over weeks; the epsom-salt and EDTA folk remedies mostly move the problem around — try them only on batteries already destined for scrap.

⚠️
Lead-acid hazards

Charging makes hydrogen — ventilate, no sparks near the top of a charging battery (connect/disconnect at the charger end). Electrolyte is sulfuric acid: eyes and skin — water, lots, immediately. The paste is lead: gloves, no food, wash up. Never fully discharge and leave flat — sulfation sets like concrete within weeks.

Lithium: harvesting 18650s

Dead laptop packs, tool packs and e-bike batteries are mostly not dead cells — usually one bad cell group or a tripped BMS kills the pack. Inside are 18650 (or 21700) cells, the best energy storage you can salvage.

1. Open carefully: Plastic shells pry apart; never puncture a cell. If any cell is dented, rusty at the terminals, leaking, or smells sweet/solvent-like — quarantine it outside, don't charge it.
2. Triage by voltage: Per cell — >2.5 V: recover with a slow charge. 2.0–2.5 V: risky; recover at very low current (50 mA) with supervision, watch for heat. <2.0 V or 0 V: discard — copper has dissolved internally and charging can create internal shorts (fire).
3. Test capacity: Charge to 4.20 V, rest a day (self-dischargers reveal themselves — discard any that drop below 4.10 V), then discharge through a known load timing the run, or use a cheap capacity tester. Keep cells ≥70% of label capacity.
4. Match and build: Group cells of similar measured capacity into parallel groups; always use a BMS sized to the pack (it balances cells and cuts off over/under-voltage). Use spot-welded nickel strip or spring holders — soldering directly to cells cooks them; if you must solder, pre-tinned tips, big iron, seconds of contact. A rewound microwave transformer (§2) makes a serviceable tab spot-welder.
5. Charge discipline: Li-ion: 4.2 V max, stop at 3.0 V, store half-charged (3.6–3.8 V) and cool. LiFePO₄ (many tool and solar packs): 3.65 V max per cell, far more forgiving and the safest lithium chemistry for a home-built bank.
🔥
Lithium thermal runaway is not a normal fire

A shorted or abused lithium cell can vent flame at 700°C+, ignite its neighbours, and water will not reliably stop it. Charge salvaged cells in a metal box or on a ceramic/brick surface, away from bedrooms, never unattended, with a bucket of dry sand as the extinguisher of choice. Never carry loose cells against keys or coins — a pocket short is a burn injury.

NiMH/NiCd (older tools, emergency lights): tolerant, simple to charge (C/10 for 14 h), often revivable from 0 V — worth salvaging for low-power radios and lights where lithium risk isn't wanted.

10. Making Fuel — The Honest Overview

Start with the truth: you cannot make petrol or diesel. They come from distilling crude oil (or industrial synthesis plants). What you can do is make substitutes for specific engines, and stretch what remains:

Fuel you can makeReplacesWorks inEffort
Biodiesel (§11)DieselAny diesel; blend in winterModerate — chemistry, methanol + lye needed
Straight veg oil (§12)DieselOlder mechanical-injection diesels, convertedLow chemistry, moderate plumbing
Ethanol (§13)Petrol (blend or high-%)Petrol engines with jetting/line changesHigh — ferment + distil at scale, legal permits
Wood gas (§14)Petrol (100%), diesel (dual-fuel)Stationary engines, adapted vehiclesHigh build effort, then fuel is free wood
Biogas (digester)Propane/natural gasSpark engines, burnersLow tech, low energy density

Stretching what's left: petrol keeps 6–12 months stabilised (§15); diesel keeps years if kept dry and treated. In a pinch, kerosene/heating oil/jet fuel run in most diesels with 2-stroke oil added (~1:200) for pump lubrication. Waste motor oil is not a diesel fuel for anything you care about — heavily filtered and blended it has been run in old, simple, indirect-injection engines, but it kills modern injection systems; treat it as a heating fuel instead.

11. Biodiesel from Waste Vegetable Oil

Transesterification turns used cooking oil into a genuine diesel substitute: methanol + lye split the glycerine off the oil molecules, leaving thin methyl-ester fuel. The bundled NREL Biodiesel Handling & Use Guide covers properties and engine use in depth.

☠️
Methoxide is the most dangerous liquid in this guide

Methanol + lye (sodium methoxide) is absorbed through skin, attacks the optic nerve, and gives no strong warning smell. Mix and pour outdoors, in HDPE containers (never glass for mixing — a dropped jar is a disaster), with gloves, goggles and no ignition sources. Methanol burns with a near-invisible flame. Keep water nearby for skin contact, and children nowhere near any of it.

1. Collect & clean the oil: Settle waste oil for days, then filter (100 µm → finer). De-water: heat to ~60°C and hold; water makes soap instead of fuel. A crackle-free test spoonful over flame = dry enough.
2. Titrate: Dissolve 1 g lye in 1 L distilled water. Mix 1 mL oil in 10 mL isopropyl alcohol with 2 drops phenolphthalein. Add lye solution drop-by-drop (counting mL) until it holds pink for 10 s. mL used = extra grams of lye needed per litre of oil.
3. Dose: Base charge 3.5 g NaOH (or 4.9 g KOH) per litre of oil + the titration extra. Methanol: 20% of oil volume (200 mL/L).
4. Make methoxide & react: Dissolve the lye fully into the methanol (swirl, patience — outdoors). Add to oil at 55–60°C and mix/pump-circulate for 1–2 h, keeping temperature.
5. Settle & separate: Glycerine (dark) sinks over 8–24 h; drain it off (it's a useful degreaser/soap feedstock — see Chemistry).
6. Wash & dry: Gently mist or bubble water through the fuel to pull out soap and methanol residue, repeating until wash water runs clear; then warm to drive off water until the fuel is bright and clear, not hazy.
7. Quality-check (27/3 test): Shake 3 mL of fuel into 27 mL methanol — a complete reaction dissolves fully with no oil dropping out. Cloudy fallout = re-react.

Using it: Any diesel runs B100 in warm weather. Biodiesel gels sooner than diesel in cold — blend (B50 → B20) or park warm in winter, and expect it to clean old fuel systems into an early filter change. It also softens old natural-rubber fuel lines (pre-~1994 vehicles) — replace with modern hose.

12. Running Diesels on Straight Vegetable Oil

Skip the chemistry: older diesels with mechanical indirect injection (classic Mercedes 240D/300D, older tractors, listers) will burn plain vegetable oil if it's hot and thin. Cold veg oil is too thick — it coke's injectors and rings.

  • Two-tank conversion (the right way): Start and shut down on diesel/biodiesel from the main tank; a second, heated tank (coolant loop through it) supplies veg oil once the engine is warm; purge back to diesel 5 minutes before shutdown so cold starts are on diesel.
  • Oil prep: settle, filter to 10 µm or better, bone dry — same as biodiesel step 1.
  • Which engines: mechanical inline/rotary-pump IDI engines tolerate it; common-rail and modern direct-injection engines do not — don't try.
  • Blending shortcut in hot climates: 10–30% clean veg oil into diesel is usually tolerated by old IDI engines even single-tank — watch smoke and power.

13. Alcohol Fuel (Ethanol)

Ethanol is a petrol-engine fuel you can genuinely farm. The bundled DOE guide Fuel from Farms is a complete plant-scale walkthrough: feedstocks, mash, fermentation, distillation, engine use.

⚖️
Legal note (today, pre-collapse)

Distilling alcohol without a licence is illegal in most countries even for fuel — in the US a free federal TTB fuel-alcohol permit exists; the UK needs an HMRC licence. Know your local law before you build a still.

  • The chain: starch/sugar feedstock (spoiled grain, potatoes, fruit, sugar beet) → cook & convert starches (malt or enzymes) → ferment with yeast to a ~10% "beer" → distil to 90%+ (the practical still limit is the 95.6% azeotrope) → dry further with 3A molecular sieve or parched corn if you need near-anhydrous for blending.
  • Engines: Petrol engines run E10–E20 with no changes. High-percentage ethanol needs richer jetting (~30–40% larger main jet area), ethanol-proof fuel lines/seals, and a cold-start aid below ~15°C (petrol priming). Ethanol's ~30% lower energy density means more fuel per hour, but octane is excellent.
  • Small-still reality: a 50 L wash at 10% yields roughly 4–5 L of usable high-proof fuel per run — meaningful for chainsaws, mowers, and generators, not for daily driving. Scale is the whole game (see the book).
  • Safety: stills concentrate flammable vapour — outdoors, flame-free heat source (or well-managed), and never run one unattended. Methanol is not a by-product of clean ethanol fermentation in dangerous amounts, but the first ~50 mL "foreshots" per run taste and burn wrong — burn them as camp fuel, don't drink any of this.

14. Wood Gas for Engines

Gasifiers turn wood into engine fuel — over a million vehicles ran on wood during WWII. The full build is covered in Energy §5, and the bundled FEMA Simplified Wood Gas Generator plans give complete drawings for a unit built from a garbage can, a drum and plumbing fittings.

  • Petrol engines run on 100% wood gas with a simple mixing valve at the intake — expect ~30–40% power loss and re-tuned timing.
  • Diesels can't ignite wood gas alone — they dual-fuel: ~70% wood gas + ~30% diesel pilot injection, no carburettor changes.
  • The catch is gas cleaning: tar and ash must be filtered (cyclone + cooler + filter) or they wreck valves — the FEMA design's filter train is the part to build carefully. And wood gas is rich in carbon monoxide: outdoor plumbing, no leaks near people.

15. Fuel Storage, Stabilising & Rotation

Core storage safety (containers, siting, quantities) is in Energy §11. The keeping-it-usable rules:

FuelUntreated lifeTreatedKeys
Petrol (E10)3–6 months12–24 months stabilisedFull sealed metal cans, cool; ethanol pulls in water — E0 stores best
Diesel1–2 years5+ yearsBiocide against diesel bug, keep tanks full (less condensation), drain water bottoms

Reviving old petrol: smell (varnish = bad), look (dark/cloudy = bad). Slightly stale petrol runs blended 1:4 with fresh in tolerant engines (mowers first, never your only generator). Gummed carburettors from stale fuel are cleaned with solvent and patience — another reason to drain carbs before storage (§6).
