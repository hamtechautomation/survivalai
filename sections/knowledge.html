📚 Knowledge & Preservation

Civilization is accumulated knowledge. In a collapse, every book, every skilled person, every teaching relationship becomes irreplaceable. Preserving and transmitting knowledge may be the most important long-term survival action your community takes.

First 24 Hours — Knowledge Priorities
  1. Locate all physical reference books — especially medical, food production, and mechanical repair.
  2. Take a skills inventory: who in your group can do what? Medical, mechanical, agricultural, electrical, construction.
  3. Secure critical documents: ID, medical records, insurance, deeds, passports — in a waterproof container.
  4. If internet is still accessible: download Wikipedia ZIM to USB now (88GB full; 22GB mini). You won't get another chance.
  5. Photograph or write down anything stored only in digital memory: passwords, contacts, account numbers, medical histories.
  6. Activate your information network — share what you know and ask what others know. Pooled knowledge saves lives.

1. Brief History of Mankind

Understanding where we came from — how humans invented agriculture, built cities, discovered science, and survived catastrophes — is not nostalgia. It is a map. Every technique that kept civilization alive through plague, collapse, and war is a technique available again. The timeline below is the condensed story of our species.

📖
Recommended Reading: The Story of Mankind

Hendrik Willem van Loon's 1921 Newbery Medal winner is the most readable one-volume history of human civilization ever written. It covers everything in the timeline below, in narrative form, with van Loon's own illustrations. Free download at gutenberg.org/ebooks/754 (EPUB — convert to PDF with Calibre and add to the PDF Library).

~300,000 BCE – 3,000 BCE · PREHISTORIC

The Stone Age & Dawn of Civilization

  • ~300,000 BCE — Homo sapiens emerges in Africa. Larger brains, language, cooperation.
  • ~1,000,000 BCE — Fire controlled and used for cooking, warmth, and protection.
  • ~70,000 BCE — Great migration out of Africa. Humans spread across Asia, Europe, and the Americas.
  • ~40,000 BCE — Art appears: cave paintings, bone flutes. Abstract thought.
  • ~10,000 BCE — Agricultural Revolution (Fertile Crescent). Wild grasses become wheat and barley. Goats, sheep, and cattle domesticated. Humanity shifts from nomadic to settled.
  • ~4,000 BCE — Wheel invented. Copper smelted. First cities appear in Sumer (Mesopotamia).
  • ~3,200 BCE — Writing invented (cuneiform, then Egyptian hieroglyphs). Knowledge can now outlast a single human life.
Survival lesson: agriculture and writing are the two foundations of civilization — both can be rebuilt from knowledge alone.
~3,000 BCE – 500 CE · ANCIENT WORLD

Empires, Philosophy & the First Collapses

  • ~3,000–1,200 BCE — Bronze Age: trade networks span continents. Egypt, Akkad, Indus Valley, and Shang China rise simultaneously — then most collapse around 1,200 BCE (Bronze Age Collapse). Causes: drought, migrations, supply chain failure.
  • ~1,200 BCE — Iron Age: iron is more abundant than tin and copper. Cheaper tools and weapons democratize power.
  • ~500 BCE — Greek Golden Age: democracy in Athens, Socrates, Plato, Aristotle. Geometry, logic, and scientific method born.
  • ~300 BCE — Alexander the Great spreads Greek knowledge from Egypt to India. Library of Alexandria founded.
  • ~200 BCE – 200 CE — Roman Empire at peak. Roads, law, aqueducts, engineering. Han Dynasty China: paper, compass, silk roads.
  • ~1–400 CE — Major religions crystallize: Buddhism spreads across Asia; Christianity spreads across Rome; early Islam emerging in Arabia.
  • 476 CE — Fall of Western Roman Empire. Roads crumble, literacy falls, trade networks collapse.
Survival lesson: the Bronze Age Collapse shows how interconnected systems fail together — and that the communities that survived were those with local food production.
500 – 1500 CE · MEDIEVAL PERIOD

Feudalism, Faith & the Islamic Golden Age

  • 500–1000 CE — Dark Ages in Western Europe: feudal agriculture, Church as repository of literacy, Roman knowledge partly preserved by monasteries and Irish monks.
  • 622 CE — Islam founded. Within 100 years, Arab armies unify territory from Spain to Central Asia.
  • ~800–1200 CE — Islamic Golden Age: algebra, optics, medicine, navigation. Greek texts translated and preserved when European libraries were burning. Scholars like Ibn Sina (medicine) and Al-Khwarizmi (algebra) advance science centuries ahead of Europe.
  • 1206–1368 CE — Mongol Empire: largest contiguous land empire in history. Silk Road trade flourishes briefly; devastation is total for cities that resist.
  • 1347–1351 CE — Black Death kills 30–50% of Europe's population. Labor becomes scarce; feudalism weakens; survivors gain bargaining power.
  • ~1440 CE — Gutenberg's printing press. Books cost 1/300th of previous price within decades. Knowledge spreads exponentially.
Survival lesson: when Western knowledge nearly died, Islamic scholars kept it alive. Knowledge survives through the people who copy it — not the institutions that hold it.
1500 – 1800 CE · EARLY MODERN

Exploration, Science & Revolution

  • 1492 — Columbus reaches the Americas. The Columbian Exchange: potatoes, maize, tomatoes, and tobacco go to Europe; smallpox, measles, and influenza devastate the Americas (90% population loss in some regions).
  • 1517 — Martin Luther's Reformation. The printing press makes it unstoppable. Religious wars follow.
  • 1543–1687 — Scientific Revolution: Copernicus, Galileo, Newton. Observation and mathematics replace authority. How the universe actually works becomes knowable.
  • 1600s — Colonial expansion. European powers extract resources from Asia, Africa, and the Americas. Global trade flows begin.
  • 1776 / 1789 — American and French Revolutions. Enlightenment ideas — rights, reason, democracy — become political realities. Monarchies start to fall.
Survival lesson: the Columbian Exchange shows how disease travels with trade. The scientific method — test, observe, revise — is humanity's most powerful tool for solving new problems.
1800 – 1945 CE · INDUSTRIAL AGE

Steam, Science & World Wars

  • ~1760–1850 — Industrial Revolution (Britain, then global): steam engines, factories, railroads. Urbanization. Life expectancy doubles in industrialized nations over 150 years.
  • 1860s — Germ theory of disease (Pasteur, Lister). Sanitation and vaccines begin eliminating ancient killers. Perhaps the single greatest improvement in human wellbeing.
  • 1876–1905 — Electricity (Edison, Tesla), telephone (Bell), automobiles (Benz), radio (Marconi), flight (Wright brothers). Modern infrastructure is born.
  • 1914–1918 — World War I: 20M dead. Machine guns, poison gas, trench warfare. Old empires collapse (Ottoman, Austro-Hungarian, Russian, German).
  • 1918–1919 — Spanish Flu pandemic: 50–100M dead worldwide — more than WWI.
  • 1929–1939 — Great Depression: global financial system collapse. Mass unemployment. Rise of fascism in Europe.
  • 1939–1945 — World War II: 70–85M dead. Holocaust. Nuclear weapons deployed. The most destructive event in human history.
Survival lesson: germ theory and sanitation — not medicine or surgery — saved the most lives. Clean water and waste separation remain the highest-value health interventions without modern infrastructure.
1945 – PRESENT · MODERN ERA

The Long Peace, Globalization & New Threats

  • 1945–1991 — Cold War: US and USSR nuclear standoff. Decolonization of Asia and Africa. Space race (Sputnik 1957; Moon landing 1969).
  • 1950s–1970s — Green Revolution: high-yield crops, fertilizers, and irrigation triple global food output. Famine largely eliminated in much of the world — but dependence on industrial inputs created.
  • 1960s–1980s — Vaccines eliminate smallpox (1980), near-eliminate polio. Global child mortality falls by 80%.
  • 1991 — Soviet Union dissolves. Brief "unipolar moment." Global trade and travel accelerate.
  • 1990s–2000s — Internet connects the world. Global information infrastructure creates unprecedented knowledge access — and unprecedented fragility.
  • 2000s–present — Climate change, antibiotic resistance, pandemic risk, and geopolitical fragmentation emerge as systemic threats. The era of infinite growth assumptions is ending.
  • 8 billion humans depend on global supply chains, industrial agriculture, and electronic infrastructure that didn't exist 80 years ago.
Survival lesson: modernity solved ancient problems by creating systemic dependencies. Understanding what came before modernity is the foundation for rebuilding if those systems fail.

2. Preserving Books & Documents

Paper is fragile. Enemies are: moisture, light, insects, fire, physical damage, and acid degradation. Books that aren't protected will be unreadable within decades.

Environmental Controls

  • Temperature: Store below 65°F (18°C). Every 10°C reduction in temperature doubles the life of paper. Even a cool basement is dramatically better than an attic.
  • Humidity: Maintain 30–50% relative humidity. High humidity promotes mold; low humidity causes brittleness. A sealed container with silica gel desiccant packs is effective.
  • Darkness: UV light degrades paper and ink faster than almost anything. Store books in closed boxes or dark containers. Never display important books in direct sunlight.
  • Acid-free materials: Standard cardboard boxes leach acid into documents over time. Use acid-free archival boxes and folders. Available from library supply companies — stock up now.

Physical Protection

  • Mylar bags: Heat-sealed Mylar (polyester film) bags with oxygen absorbers provide decades of protection for individual documents and photos.
  • Lamination: Laminate frequently-referenced documents (maps, quick reference cards, checklists) for water and tear resistance. Use 5-mil or heavier laminate for durability.
  • Book repair: Learn basic bookbinding repair — tightening loose signatures, rebacking torn spines, and reinforcing worn hinges extends a book's life by decades. Materials: bone folder, PVA glue, bookcloth, binder's thread.
  • Fire protection: Store duplicates in multiple separate locations. No single storage location is truly fireproof. A fireproof safe provides some protection but is not a substitute for geographic redundancy.

Pest Control

  • Bookworms (larvae of various beetles) and silverfish can devastate a library. Keep storage areas clean and dry — pests prefer humid, dusty environments.
  • Cedar blocks, dried lavender, and cloves naturally repel insects. Replace every 6 months.
  • Inspect stored books annually. Any evidence of insect damage: isolate the affected volume, freeze it for 72 hours at below -20°C (-4°F) to kill insects and eggs, then clean and rehouse.

4. Community Library Setup

Organization

  • Classification: Use a simplified Dewey Decimal or subject-based system. Main categories: Medicine, Agriculture/Food, Engineering/Construction, Science, Law/Governance, History/Philosophy, Skills & Crafts, Fiction/General.
  • Catalog: Maintain a written ledger with: title, author, subject category, shelf location, and brief description. This catalog becomes the index to your library when someone needs to find "anything about plant diseases."
  • Location: Dedicated, weatherproof space. A room with minimal windows, stable temperature, and a lockable door. Keep away from food preparation (moisture, insects, smells).

Lending System

  • A simple sign-out ledger: book title, borrower's name, date out, date due, date returned.
  • Loan period: 2 weeks standard, 1 week for high-demand references.
  • Designate reference books that don't leave the library — these are too critical to risk loss or damage.
  • Repair policy: borrowers responsible for damage beyond normal wear. Community agreement on what constitutes acceptable treatment.

Building the Collection

  • Start now — download and print key references. Free sources: Project Gutenberg (public domain books), Internet Archive, WikiHow, offline Wikipedia dumps (available at kiwix.org).
  • Accept donations but curate — not everything is worth storing. Prioritize practical and reference over entertainment (entertainment matters for morale but is lower priority for space).
  • Duplicate the most critical references — multiple copies of Where There Is No Doctor, key agricultural references, and this guide.

5. Teaching & Apprenticeship Systems

Books transmit knowledge. People transmit skills. The difference matters — reading about suturing a wound is not the same as having sutured a wound under supervision. For skills that require physical practice, apprenticeship (learning by doing alongside an expert) is irreplaceable.

Skill Inventory

First step: map the skills present in your community.

  • Survey every member: what do they do professionally? What do they do recreationally? What did their parents or grandparents do?
  • Document: medical (nurses, EMTs, doctors, midwives), agricultural, construction, mechanical, electronics, cooking/preservation, navigation, communications, law/governance, teaching.
  • Identify single points of failure — skills held by only one person. Prioritize training a backup immediately.

Traditional Apprenticeship Structure

PhaseDurationActivity
ObservationWeek 1–2Apprentice watches master work without interrupting. Takes notes. Asks questions only after sessions.
Assisted practiceWeeks 3–8Apprentice does simple tasks under constant supervision. Master corrects immediately. Safety is paramount.
Supervised practiceMonths 3–6Apprentice does most work; master observes and corrects. Apprentice takes initiative but master remains present.
Independent practiceMonths 7+Apprentice works independently with master available for consultation. Begins teaching the next apprentice.

Teaching Adults

  • Adult learners need to know why before they will fully engage with how. Always explain the reason for each step.
  • Learning transfers best when emotionally significant. Teaching wound care to someone who has recently needed it is highly effective.
  • Spaced repetition: a skill practiced once is mostly forgotten. Practice the same skill multiple times over days and weeks.
  • Teach-back: have the learner immediately teach what they just learned to another person. This is the fastest way to reveal gaps in understanding.

Documentation — Create Teaching Materials

  • Every skilled person should document their knowledge: step-by-step procedures with illustrations, common mistakes, safety warnings, and required materials.
  • Materials don't need to be polished — handwritten, hand-illustrated guides are often better than printed materials because learners can see that someone like them created it.
  • These materials go into the community library and can outlast the original teacher.

6. Record Keeping Without Computers

In a post-grid society, maintaining records of births, deaths, property, agreements, inventories, and accounts becomes a community function. Without records, property disputes multiply, agreements are forgotten, and community history is lost.

Essential Records to Maintain

  • Vital records: Births (date, parents, location), deaths (date, cause, location), marriages, and adoptions.
  • Property records: Land boundaries (reference surveyed landmarks), improvements, transfers, agreements, disputes resolved.
  • Community agreements: Rules, constitutions, laws. Any agreement that has community force should be written, dated, and witnessed.
  • Inventory: Community resource inventory — what do we have, where is it, how much. Updated monthly minimum.
  • Medical records: Individuals' medical histories, allergies, medications, and treatments received.
  • Skills registry: Who can do what — the community skills inventory.

Record Keeping Materials

  • Paper: Acid-free archival paper lasts centuries if stored properly. Stock it now. Standard office paper is acceptable for records you'll need to re-copy every 10–20 years.
  • Ink: Archival-quality iron gall ink lasts indefinitely. Ballpoint pen ink is adequate for decades. Pencil fades and can be erased — avoid for permanent records.
  • Ledger books: Bound ledgers (not spiral-bound or loose-leaf) are preferred. Pages are sequential and difficult to remove without evidence.
  • Improvised paper: Paper can be made from plant fibers: cotton, flax, hemp, straw. Requires a mold and deckle, water, and sun-drying. Production is time-intensive but feasible.
  • Charcoal/berry ink: Improvised inks from charcoal paste, berry juice (unstable, fades fast), walnut hulls (brown, fairly stable), and oak galls + iron (black, very durable — the same iron gall ink used in medieval manuscripts).

Double-Entry System for Inventories

Adapted from accounting: every resource that comes in or goes out is recorded in two places. This creates redundancy and makes errors detectable.

7. Archiving Digital Data Offline

Why Digital Archiving Matters

Digital data — Wikipedia, technical manuals, medical databases, maps, this survival guide — contains more practical knowledge than could fit in any physical library. The challenge: digital storage requires powered devices to read it. A USB drive is useless without a working computer.

Essential Offline Digital Libraries

  • Kiwix + offline Wikipedia: Kiwix is free software; download it and the full English Wikipedia ZIM file (88GB compressed, or 23GB without images). Includes all of Wikipedia's knowledge, readable without internet.
  • Project Gutenberg: 70,000+ free public domain books. Download the full catalog on DVD or USB.
  • iFixit: Offline repair guides for electronics, appliances, vehicles.
  • Khan Academy Lite: Offline educational courses. Full curriculum for all ages.
  • OpenStreetMap: Offline maps. Download your region using OsmAnd (Android/iOS) before a crisis. Stores as files that work offline.
  • This survival guide: Already offline — distribute on USB drives to all community members.

Power for Reading Digital Data

A solar-charged power bank can run a Raspberry Pi or low-power laptop indefinitely — making digital archives perpetually accessible even without grid power.

  • Raspberry Pi 4 (consumes 3–7W): runs Linux, browsers, offline Wikipedia, all office applications. Connect to any monitor or TV. Stock spare units in a Faraday cage.
  • Kindle e-reader: consumes ~0.3W when reading. Lasts weeks on a single charge. Load with ebooks and PDFs — your entire library in one hand.
  • Charge devices before battery depletion — deeply discharged lithium batteries degrade faster.

8. Essential Skills to Teach Children

Children are survivors. They adapt rapidly, learn without the resistance adults often show, and will carry skills and values into the next generation. Teaching children useful skills is one of the highest-return investments a community can make.

Age-Appropriate Skill Introduction

Age GroupSkills to IntroduceNotes
3–6 yearsPlant ID (safe plants, "don't touch without asking"), handwashing and hygiene, fire safety awareness ("hot, don't touch"), animal care basicsFocus on safety awareness and simple responsibility. Make it playful.
7–10 yearsFire starting (supervised), basic first aid (STOP bleeding — apply pressure), cooking simple foods, gardening, knots (square knot, bowline), reading a compassResponsibility builds confidence. Give real tasks, not pretend ones.
11–14 yearsNavigation with map and compass, food preservation (jam, dehydration), basic construction (measuring, cutting, hammering), water purification, foraging basics, radio operation basicsChildren this age can learn adult skills with supervision. Apprentice them to skilled adults.
15+ yearsFull apprenticeship in community skills, emergency first aid, community governance participation, teaching younger children, leadership in supervised contextsTeenagers are capable of adult responsibilities. Treat them as junior adults, not children.

The Most Important Things to Teach Any Child

  • Critical thinking: "How do you know that's true?" and "What would happen if you're wrong?" — more valuable than any specific factual knowledge.
  • How to learn: Using books, asking questions, observing, practicing — the skill of learning any skill.
  • Basic literacy and numeracy: Reading, writing, and arithmetic are prerequisites for everything else. Prioritize these above all other formal instruction.
  • Community values: Cooperation, fairness, honesty, and care for the vulnerable. Communities that teach and live these values outperform those that don't, in every historical and modern context.
  • Physical resilience: Walking, carrying, swimming, climbing — basic physical competence. Children who are comfortable with physical challenge adapt better to difficult conditions.
  • Emotional regulation: How to manage fear, anger, and grief. Skills that help children (and adults) function under stress.

Maintaining Education Through Crisis

  • Establish a regular school schedule as quickly as possible after a crisis. Routine is psychologically stabilizing for children.
  • Use any literate adult as a teacher. Subject matter expertise is less important than organized, consistent learning time.
  • Integrate practical skills with academic learning — math through measuring and construction; science through observation of plants, water, and fire.
  • Use storytelling to teach history, values, and culture. Stories are the oldest and most reliable knowledge-transmission technology.
  • Don't forget play: Play is how children process experience, develop socially, and maintain mental health. Even in severe crisis, protected time for unstructured play matters enormously.
✅
The One-Room Schoolhouse Model

Multi-age classrooms (one teacher with children 6–14) are more effective than age-segregated classes for several skills: older students reinforce their own learning by teaching younger students. Peer learning multiplies the teacher's effectiveness. Mixed-age collaboration mirrors the adult world children are preparing for. One competent teacher in a structured one-room model can educate 20–30 children effectively.
